Vocabulary

Key Terms & Definitions

Master these geographical concepts to understand how we study places and environments.

Geography
The study of places and the relationships between people and their environments. Geographers explore both physical features of Earth and human societies spread across it.
Place
Parts of the Earth's surface identified and given meaning by people. Places can be natural (shaped by environment) or built (constructed by humans).
Space
How things are mapped and arranged on Earth's surface, including location, spatial distribution (patterns), and organization.
Environment
The world we live in, made up of natural environments (deserts, forests, oceans) and built environments (cities, towns, farmland).
Interconnection
Complex links between natural and human processes. Everything on Earth is connected â€” changes in one area affect others.
Sustainability
Using and managing Earth's resources responsibly so they're available for future generations.
Scale
The spatial level at which we study geography: local (neighborhood), regional, national, international, or global.
Change
How Earth is constantly changing through natural processes (climate, disasters) or human activities. Changes can be rapid or take millions of years.
Liveability
How suitable and enjoyable a place is to live in. Depends on factors like climate, infrastructure, safety, environmental quality, and access to services.
Objective Factors
Measurable aspects of liveability expressed as numbers: housing costs, crime rates, number of hospitals, climate data.
Subjective Factors
Personal, emotional, spiritual aspects of liveability that can't be easily measured: spiritual connections, sentimental attachments, personal preferences.
Infrastructure
Services and facilities like roads, public transport, emergency services, water, sewage, electricity, housing, and communications.
Demographics
Statistical data about populations: age, education, income, cultural background.
Climate
Long-term weather patterns in an area. Most people find temperate (mild) climates most liveable.
Accessibility
How easy it is to reach services and facilities. Includes ability to walk, cycle, or use public transport.
Grid Reference (GR)
A six-figure reference to pinpoint an exact location on a map. Format: GR + 3 easting numbers + 3 northing numbers.
Area Reference (AR)
A four-figure reference to find a general area on a map. Format: AR + easting number + northing number.

What is PACER? How Do I Study for High School?

PACER Method

How to Remember Everything You Read

PACER is a study method that helps you retain information from textbooks, articles, and notes. It stands for:

  • Preview â€” Skim the material first. Read headings, subheadings, bold terms, and summaries to get an overview.
  • Annotate â€” Actively mark up the text. Highlight key points, write notes in margins, circle important terms.
  • Connect â€” Link new information to what you already know. Ask yourself: How does this relate to other topics?
  • Elaborate â€” Explain concepts in your own words. Teach it to someone else or write a summary.
  • Review â€” Go back over your notes regularly. Spaced repetition (reviewing multiple times) locks information into long-term memory.

Why it works: PACER transforms passive reading into active learning. Your brain remembers information better when you engage with it multiple ways â€” seeing it, writing it, explaining it, and reviewing it.

What are SPICESS?

7 Concepts

The SPICESS Framework

SPICESS is an acronym for the seven key geographical concepts we use to understand the world. Every geographic inquiry uses these concepts to investigate and analyze places and environments.

S â€” Space

Where things are located and how they're arranged. Includes patterns, distribution, and organization of features on Earth's surface.

P â€” Place

Parts of Earth identified and given meaning by people. Can be natural or built. Influences our relationships and how we live.

I â€” Interconnection

Links between natural and human processes. Everything on Earth is connected â€” changes in one place affect others.

C â€” Change

Earth is constantly changing through natural processes and human activity. Changes can be rapid or gradual, positive or negative.

E â€” Environment

Natural environments (deserts, forests, oceans) and built environments (cities, towns, farms). Most places are now a combination of both.

S â€” Sustainability

Using resources responsibly so they're available for future generations. Balancing present needs with long-term environmental health.

S â€” Scale

The spatial level of study: local (neighborhood), regional, national, international, or global. Same topic can be studied at different scales.

Week 3

What are BOLTSS & Grid Referencing?

Map Features

BOLTSS â€” Essential Features of Every Map

Every good map needs these six features to be clear, accurate, and useful. Remember them with the acronym BOLTSS:

  • B â€” Border: An outline or box drawn around the map to contain all information.
  • O â€” Orientation: Shows direction, usually with a north arrow or compass rose.
  • L â€” Legend (Key): Explains symbols, colors, and patterns used on the map.
  • T â€” Title: Describes what the map shows and the area it covers.
  • S â€” Scale: Shows how distances on the map relate to real-world distances. Can be written, line, or ratio scale.
  • S â€” Source: Where the map information came from.
Map Skills

Types of Maps

Geographers use different types of maps for different purposes:

  • Physical Maps: Show natural features like mountains, rivers, deserts, and oceans.
  • Political Maps: Show built features like country borders, cities, and towns.
  • Topographic Maps: Show land shape using contour lines. Closer lines = steeper land.
  • Thematic Maps: Focus on specific themes like population, climate, or resources.
  • Choropleth Maps: Use color shading to show data patterns (darker = more).
Location Skills

Grid Referencing

Area Reference (AR): Four-figure reference to find a general area.

  • Find the square the object is in
  • Put a dot at the bottom-right corner
  • Format: AR + easting number + northing number
  • Example: AR 2813

Grid Reference (GR): Six-figure reference to pinpoint exact locations.

  • Find which grid square the object is in
  • Divide that square into 10 more lines (imagine or use ruler)
  • Count eastings (across) then northings (up)
  • Format: GR + 3 easting numbers + 3 northing numbers
  • Example: GR 176 616

Memory trick: "In the house, up the stairs" â€” Go across (eastings) FIRST, then up (northings).

Week 4

How Do We Measure Liveability?

Key Concept

What is Liveability?

Liveability is how suitable and enjoyable a place is to live in. What makes a place "liveable" depends on your wants and needs â€” age, income, cultural background, lifestyle, values, and beliefs all influence what you find liveable.

Features of a liveable place:

  • Offers a temperate (mild) climate
  • Easy to get around
  • Good healthcare, work, and education opportunities
  • Safe and stable
  • Affordable
  • Diverse
  • Sustainable
  • Attractive
Measurement

Objective vs Subjective Factors

Objective Factors (Measurable)

Things that can be measured and expressed as numbers:

  • Climate: Temperature, rainfall data
  • Environmental Quality: Air/water quality, pollution levels, noise
  • Infrastructure: Number of hospitals, schools, transport options
  • Safety & Stability: Crime statistics, police presence
  • Access to Services: Distance to healthcare, education, amenities

Organizations like the Economist Intelligence Unit (EIU) rank cities worldwide using objective factors.

Subjective Factors (Personal & Emotional)

Things that cannot be easily measured:

  • Spiritual Connections: Deep relationships with particular places (especially important for Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander peoples)
  • Sentimental Appeal: Places attached to memories and experiences
  • Aesthetic Appeal: How a place looks and feels
  • Personal Preferences: Individual likes and dislikes
  • Connections to People: Proximity to family, friends, cultural groups

The OECD now conducts life-satisfaction surveys to capture subjective factors alongside objective measures.

Analysis

Why We Live Where We Do

Three key questions help us understand liveability:

1. What do you like to do?

Access to activities you enjoy affects liveability. Surfers prefer coastal towns. Horse riders need rural areas with open spaces.

2. Where do you like to go?

How easy is it to travel to places you want to be? Can you walk, bike, or use public transport? Travel difficulty decreases liveability.

3. What are your favourite places?

Places can be special for aesthetic, sentimental, or spiritual reasons. These connections increase a place's liveability for you specifically.
