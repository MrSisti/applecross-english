Ender's Game

by Orson Scott Card

A novel about a child genius recruited into a militarist institution to fight an alien war — and everything Card is actually doing with that premise. This guide breaks down the six representative ideas Card builds into the text, and why they matter for your analysis.

01 — Overview

The Text & Its Context

What Card was doing when he wrote this, and why it matters before you start analysing.

About the Author

Orson Scott Card

Card grew up reading Asimov and Gibbon's Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire. His Mormon upbringing shaped his thinking about community, duty, and moral obligation. He was not anti-Vietnam — he saw the military as patriotic, partly from reading Catton's Army of the Potomac. This matters: Card isn't writing from a position of straightforward anti-war sentiment. He's writing about how power works on people who believe they're serving something larger than themselves.

Card has an anti-intellectual streak. He's called archaeology a "semi-science" and dismissed literary critics who read complexity into his work. This tension — between what Card says he's doing and what the text actually does — is useful for your analysis.

Card's Stated Purpose

Writing Children as People

Card wanted to write a novel that captured the alienating experience of being a child in an adult world. He said: "Children are a perpetual, self-renewing underclass, helpless to escape from the decisions of adults until they become adults themselves."

He learned that history is shaped by the use of power — that great military leaders impose their will and the army becomes an extension of them. He wanted to make children feel like adults on the page. The question for your analysis: does the text do what Card says it does, or does it reveal something he didn't intend?

What Card Says the Novel Is About

The Big Themes

  • The alienation of childhood and the cost of genius
  • Fascism, militarism, and how institutions shape identity
  • Xenophobia and the construction of 'the enemy'
  • Collectivism versus individualism
  • Colonialism and manifest destiny
  • The ethics of war and moral responsibility
What the Text Actually Does

Six Representative Ideas

The six representative ideas are the analytical framework for this unit. They're not simply "themes" — they're specific ways Card constructs representations of power, identity, and control through language, character, and narrative structure. Each one gives you a concrete angle for your essays. Scroll down to see them in detail.
